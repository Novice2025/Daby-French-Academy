My Method

The 'LEGO Block' Approach

We've all been there: getting bogged down in grammar on the first day and feeling like you'll never actually speak the language. That's why I've developed a unique teaching style inspired by the Alliance Française methodology and adapted for practical, real-world communication.

Instead of focusing on grammar rules, we start with functional, "ready-to-use" phrases—your LEGO blocks. You'll build confidence by using these simple, 3-4 word phrases to form sentences right from our first class. As we progress, we'll connect the blocks and uncover the grammar behind them, but the focus will always be on speaking.

  • Speaking from Day 1: Start with simple greetings like "Bonjour" and "Ça va?" to immediately build confidence.
  • CEFR-Based Progression: Our curriculum is aligned with the globally recognized Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR). We focus on what you can do with the language, not just what you know about it.
  • Interactive Classes: We use pair work and role-playing to get you talking to your classmates and simulating real-life scenarios like ordering at a café or asking for directions.
Class Structure

A Peek Inside Our Classes

Every 60-90 minute class is designed to keep you engaged and speaking French. Here's a look at what a typical session looks like:

  1. Warm-up (5-10 min): We'll begin with quick, fun questions to get you speaking and recycling expressions from our last class.
  2. New Expressions (15-20 min): You'll learn 3-4 new "LEGO blocks" in context, like "Je m'appelle..." or "Je voudrais un café..."
  3. Controlled Practice (15 min): You'll work with your classmates on guided dialogues and mini role-plays to practice the new expressions.
  4. Expansion (10-15 min): We'll add one small twist—a new word or a simple grammar note—to expand on the blocks you've learned.
  5. Wrap-up & Review (5-10 min): We'll finish with a quick recap, where you'll get to tell me and your classmates what you learned, all in French!
Syllabus

Think of this as your first season of a binge-worthy show. Each "episode" is a 1-hour class designed to build on the last, so you can go from zero to conversational.

Episode 1: Bonjour & First Contacts

Objective: By the end of this class, you will be able to greet someone, introduce yourself, and ask for their name in a simple conversation.

  • Greetings & Farewells: Bonjour (Hello), Bonsoir (Good evening), Au revoir (Goodbye), Salut! (Hi/Bye), À bientôt (See you soon).
  • Introductions: Ça va? (How are you?), Oui, ça va. (I'm fine.), Comme ci, comme ça. (So-so.), Pas mal. (Not bad.), Comment tu t’appelles ? (What's your name?), Je m’appelle… (My name is…), Et toi ? (And you?), Enchanté(e) (Nice to meet you).
  • Courtesy: S'il te plaît (Please - informal), Merci (Thank you), De rien (You're welcome), Excusez-moi (Excuse me), Pardon (Sorry).

Dialogue Example:
A: Salut! Ça va?
B: Oui, ça va, merci! Et toi?
A: Ça va bien. Je m'appelle Marie. Et toi?
B: Je m'appelle Paul. Enchanté!
A: Enchantée!

Cultural Note: In French, using "bonjour" is very important and should be said to everyone you greet, even strangers. Not saying "bonjour" can be considered rude. There are also formal and informal ways of speaking, like "tu" and "vous."

Task: You will engage in a "speed-dating" style mingle with your classmates. You'll move around the room, introducing yourself and getting to know at least five new people, using only the new phrases.

Episode 2: Where Are You From?

Objective: You will be able to ask someone about their nationality and where they live, and you will respond with your own. You will also learn the difference between feminine and masculine forms for nationalities.

  • Nationalities: Tu es de quelle nationalité ? (What nationality are you?), Je suis brésilien(ne), français(e), anglais(e), américain(e), chinois(e), mexicain(e), espagnol(e), allemand(e).
  • Where you live: Tu habites où ? (Where do you live?), J’habite à São Paulo, Paris, Londres, New York, Tokyo, Madrid, Berlin.
  • Useful words: La ville (city), le pays (country), le continent (continent), la langue (language), la capitale (capital).

Dialogue Example:
A: Tu es de quelle nationalité?
B: Je suis brésilienne. Et toi?
A: Je suis américain. Tu habites où?
B: J'habite à São Paulo.

Cultural Note: In French, adjectives like nationalities must agree in gender with the person they describe. For example, "américain" is for a male, and "américaine" is for a female. It's a key part of French grammar.

Task: You will interview a partner to create a profile for them, including their name, nationality, and city of residence. Then, you'll present your partner to the class using your new knowledge.

Episode 3: Numbers & Contact Details

Objective: By the end of this class, you will be able to count from 0 to 20 and ask for and give out simple contact information like a phone number or email address.

  • Numbers (0-20): Zéro, un, deux, trois, quatre, cinq, six, sept, huit, neuf, dix, onze, douze, treize, quatorze, quinze, seize, dix-sept, dix-huit, dix-neuf, vingt.
  • Contact Details: Quel est ton numéro de téléphone ? (What's your phone number?), C’est le… (It's…), Quelle est ton adresse mail ? (What's your email address?), C'est…@...point…fr/com.
  • Useful words: Un numéro (a number), une adresse (an address), un portable (a cell phone), un e-mail (an email).

Dialogue Example:
A: Quel est ton numéro de téléphone?
B: C'est le 06 12 34 56 78.
A: Merci! Et ton adresse mail?
B: C'est paul@gmail.com.

Cultural Note: When giving out a phone number in France, people often group the numbers in pairs (e.g., 06-12-34-56-78). This is different from how it's done in many other countries and can be a bit tricky at first!

Task: You will play a "French detective" game. You'll receive a mock "case file" with a phone number or email address written as a puzzle. You'll need to say the numbers out loud to your partner to decode the information.

Episode 4: Café Survival

Objective: You will be able to order food and drinks politely in a café and ask about prices. You will learn key phrases for a real-life situation.

  • Drinks: Un café, un thé, un jus d'orange, une bouteille d'eau, un verre de vin, une bière, un chocolat chaud, une limonade.
  • Food: Un croissant, une baguette, un sandwich, une salade, une soupe, un gâteau.
  • Ordering: Je voudrais… (I would like…), Je prends… (I'll take…), Pour moi, ce sera… (For me, it will be…), S’il vous plaît (Please - formal), Ça fait combien ? (How much is it?), Ça fait… euros (It's… euros).
  • Other: Une carte, un menu (a menu), l'addition (the bill), un pourboire (a tip), le serveur/la serveuse (the waiter/waitress).

Dialogue Example:
A: Bonjour, je voudrais un café, s'il vous plaît.
B: Bonjour! Ça fait 2 euros.
A: Voici. Merci. Au revoir!
B: Au revoir!

Cultural Note: In France, there's a distinction between "un café" (a small espresso) and "un café au lait" or "un grand café" if you want a larger coffee. Tipping is not as common as in other places, as a service charge is usually included in the bill.

Task: You will role-play as a waiter and a customer in a simulated Parisian café. You will practice taking and giving orders, asking about prices, and responding politely.

Episode 5: Classroom Language

Objective: You will be able to manage basic classroom interactions in French, empowering you to ask for help and clarify instructions without switching back to English.

  • Questions: Je peux aller aux toilettes ? (May I go to the bathroom?), Répétez, s'il vous plaît. (Repeat, please.), J’ai une question. (I have a question.), Je ne comprends pas. (I don't understand.), Ça veut dire quoi ? (What does that mean?), Comment on dit... en français ? (How do you say... in French?).
  • Instructions: Écoutez-moi (Listen to me), Regardez le tableau (Look at the board), Lisez la page... (Read page...), Écrivez (Write), Répétez (Repeat), Parlez plus lentement (Speak more slowly).
  • Useful words: Un mot (a word), une phrase (a sentence), un exercice (an exercise), un dictionnaire (a dictionary), la page (the page), l'ordinateur (the computer).

Dialogue Example:
Teacher: Lisez la page 10, s'il vous plaît.
Student: Pardon, je ne comprends pas. Pouvez-vous répéter, s'il vous plaît?
Teacher: Oui, bien sûr. Lisez la page 10.

Cultural Note: In a French classroom, it is common to use the formal "vous" with the teacher, even if they are very friendly. This shows respect for their position. Using phrases like "s'il vous plaît" is essential for politeness.

Task: Your teacher will only give instructions in French! You will need to use your new expressions to follow along and ask for clarification when needed, giving you the confidence to communicate in a French-only environment.

Episode 6: Family Basics

Objective: By the end of this class, you will be able to present your family members and describe them with simple adjectives like "big" or "small."

  • Family Members: La famille (family), un frère (a brother), une sœur (a sister), un fils (a son), une fille (a daughter), mon père (my father), ma mère (my mother), mes parents (my parents), mon mari (my husband), ma femme (my wife), un oncle (an uncle), une tante (an aunt), un grand-père (a grandfather), une grand-mère (a grandmother), un enfant (a child), un ami (a friend).
  • Descriptions: Grand(e) (tall/big), petit(e) (small/short), jeune (young), vieux/vieille (old), beau/belle (beautiful), gentil(le) (nice), drôle (funny), sympa (nice), intelligent(e) (intelligent), timide (shy).
  • Possession: Voici ma famille (Here is my family), J'ai un frère (I have a brother), Il/Elle s'appelle... (His/Her name is…).

Dialogue Example:
A: Voici ma famille.
B: C'est qui?
A: C'est ma sœur. Elle est très intelligente et sympa.
B: Elle est belle!

Cultural Note: Family is very important in French culture. Many conversations revolve around "la famille." It's common to ask about someone's family as a way of getting to know them better.

Task: You will create a "family tree" on the whiteboard, using names and photos (or simple drawings) to introduce 2-3 of your family members to a partner. You'll answer follow-up questions about them.

Episode 7: Jobs & Workplace

Objective: You will learn to talk about your job and profession, or what you study, and ask others about theirs. You'll gain the confidence to network in French.

  • Professions: Vous faites quoi dans la vie ? (What do you do for a living?), Je suis étudiant(e), professeur(e), médecin, ingénieur(e), artiste, chef cuisinier(e), journaliste, avocat(e), policier(e), pompier(e).
  • Workplace: Je travaille chez... (I work at...), Je suis au chômage. (I'm unemployed.), Je cherche un emploi. (I'm looking for a job.), Le bureau (the office), une entreprise (a company).
  • Studies: J'étudie… (I study…), L'université (the university), l'école (the school).

Dialogue Example:
A: Vous faites quoi dans la vie?
B: Je suis professeur. Et vous?
A: Je suis étudiant. J'étudie la musique.
B: C'est intéressant!

Cultural Note: It's common for French people to use the formal "vous" when asking about someone's job, especially in a professional or formal setting. The question "Vous faites quoi dans la vie?" is a polite and common way to ask about someone's profession.

Task: You will participate in a mock job fair. You'll give a 30-second professional introduction to your classmates, and they will ask you follow-up questions about your role and responsibilities.

Episode 8: Daily Routine & Time

Objective: You will be able to describe your daily routine and specific hours, giving you the ability to tell someone how you spend your day.

  • Daily Routine Verbs: Je me réveille (I wake up), Je me lève (I get up), je me douche (I shower), je prends mon petit-déjeuner (I have my breakfast), je vais au travail (I go to work), je déjeune (I have lunch), je dîne (I have dinner), je me couche (I go to bed).
  • Time & Frequency: À quelle heure... ? (At what time?), Le matin (in the morning), l'après-midi (in the afternoon), le soir (in the evening), la nuit (at night), tous les jours (every day), le lundi, le mardi... (on Mondays, on Tuesdays...).
  • Expressions: Je finis le travail à dix-huit heures (I finish work at 6 PM), Je me lève tôt/tard (I get up early/late), J'ai un emploi du temps chargé (I have a busy schedule).

Dialogue Example:
A: Tu te lèves à quelle heure?
B: Je me lève à sept heures. Et toi?
A: Moi, je me lève à six heures et je vais au travail à huit heures.
B: C'est tôt!

Cultural Note: French meals, especially dinner, are often later than in other countries. Dinner is typically around 8 PM, and lunch is usually between 12 PM and 2 PM. There is a strong focus on taking a proper lunch break, not just eating at your desk.

Task: You'll create a simple timeline of your day. You'll present your routine to a partner, and they'll try to guess what time you do certain activities.

Episode 9: Dates & Making Plans

Objective: By the end of this class, you will know the days of the week and be able to arrange meetings and outings with friends in French.

  • Days of the Week: Lundi, mardi, mercredi, jeudi, vendredi, samedi, dimanche.
  • Making Plans: On se voit quand ? (When do we see each other?), Êtes-vous disponible... ? (Are you available...?), Je suis libre. (I'm free.), Je suis occupé(e). (I'm busy.), On va au cinéma ? (Shall we go to the cinema?), Oui, bonne idée. (Yes, good idea.), Je ne peux pas. (I can't.).
  • Time Expressions: Ce week-end (this weekend), la semaine prochaine (next week), ce soir (this evening), demain (tomorrow), après-demain (the day after tomorrow), la semaine dernière (last week).

Dialogue Example:
A: On se voit ce week-end?
B: Bonne idée! Tu es libre samedi soir?
A: Non, je suis occupé. Mais je suis libre dimanche après-midi.
B: D'accord, dimanche après-midi!

Cultural Note: It's common to greet someone with "bonjour" even if you are meeting to make plans. French conversations about plans are typically direct and to the point, so you'll get straight to the "when" and "where."

Task: You will plan two different appointments with a classmate. One will be a coffee on a specific day, and the other will be a movie night on a different day. You will need to confirm the day and time with them.

Episode 10: Directions & Places

Objective: You will be able to ask for and give simple directions to a place, empowering you to navigate a French city with confidence.

  • Asking for Directions: Où est la gare ? (Where is the train station?), Comment je vais à...? (How do I get to...?), C'est près/loin ? (Is it near/far?).
  • Giving Directions: Allez tout droit (Go straight ahead), Tournez à gauche/droite (Turn left/right), Continuez jusqu'à... (Continue until...), Prenez la première/deuxième rue à droite (Take the first/second street on the right), C'est en face de... (It's opposite...).
  • Places: Le supermarché, la boulangerie (bakery), la poste (post office), la pharmacie (pharmacy), le restaurant, le cinéma, le musée, la banque, l'hôtel.

Dialogue Example:
A: Excusez-moi, où est la boulangerie?
B: La boulangerie? Allez tout droit, puis tournez à droite. C'est en face de la poste.
A: Merci beaucoup!
B: De rien.

Cultural Note: When asking for directions in France, it's polite to start with "Excusez-moi" (formal) or "Excuse-moi" (informal). Similarly, when someone helps you, you should always say "merci beaucoup."

Task: You will work in pairs with a simple map of a fictional town. One person will be the tourist asking for directions to specific places, and the other will be the local giving the directions.

Episode 11: Home & Housing

Objective: You will be able to describe your home, the rooms in it, and basic furniture, preparing you for conversations about where you live.

  • Rooms: Une maison (a house), un appartement (an apartment), une chambre (a bedroom), un salon (a living room), une cuisine (a kitchen), une salle de bain (a bathroom), la cave (the cellar), le jardin (the garden), le balcon (the balcony).
  • Furniture: Une table (a table), une chaise (a chair), un lit (a bed), un canapé (a sofa), une télévision (a TV), une armoire (a wardrobe), un bureau (a desk).
  • Descriptions: C'est grand/petit (It's big/small), moderne/ancien (modern/old), confortable (comfortable), lumineux (bright), il y a... (there is/are...).

Dialogue Example:
A: Tu habites dans une maison ou un appartement?
B: J'habite dans une maison. C'est petit mais confortable. Il y a deux chambres.
A: C'est bien! Et il y a un jardin?
B: Non, mais il y a un balcon.

Cultural Note: In France, apartments in large cities often have balconies instead of gardens. Older buildings are very common, so it's not unusual to live in a building that is centuries old. The cellar ("la cave") is also a common feature for storing wine or other items.

Task: You'll be given a set of furniture cards. You will have to describe your dream home to a partner, "placing" the furniture in different rooms using the new vocabulary you've learned.

Episode 12: Shopping & Prices

Objective: You will be able to shop for everyday items, ask for the price, and say whether something is expensive or cheap.

  • Shopping verbs: Je cherche... (I'm looking for...), Je voudrais... (I would like...), Je prends... (I'll take...), J'achète... (I buy...).
  • Prices: Ça coûte combien ? (How much does it cost?), C'est trop cher / bon marché. (It's too expensive / cheap.), Le prix (the price), l'argent (money), un euro (a euro), une réduction (a discount).
  • Shops & Places: Un supermarché, une boutique, un marché, une boulangerie, une pharmacie, la caisse (the checkout), la vitrine (the display window).
  • Payment: Je voudrais payer par carte/en espèces. (I would like to pay by card/in cash.), La carte bancaire (the bank card).

Dialogue Example:
A: Je cherche une jupe rouge.
B: C'est celle-ci? Elle coûte 50 euros.
A: Oh, c'est trop cher! Vous avez une réduction?
B: Non, désolé.

Cultural Note: Many French towns have a weekly market ("le marché") where you can buy fresh produce and other items directly from producers. It's a great place to practice your French and immerse yourself in local culture.

Task: You will be a customer at a French market. You'll have a list of items to buy and a mock budget. You'll need to negotiate prices and select what you want to purchase.

Episode 13: Likes & Dislikes

Objective: You will learn how to express your simple tastes and preferences about food, music, and movies. You will also learn to agree and disagree with others' opinions.

  • Preferences: J'aime / J'adore... (I like / I love...), Je n'aime pas / Je déteste... (I don't like / I hate...), Je préfère... (I prefer...).
  • Activities & Hobbies: La musique, le sport, les films, la lecture, la cuisine, les voyages, les jeux vidéo.
  • Agreement/Disagreement: Moi aussi. (Me too.), Moi non plus. (Me neither.), Pas moi. (Not me.), C'est super! (It's great!), C'est nul. (It's rubbish.).

Dialogue Example:
A: J'adore le chocolat! Et toi?
B: Moi aussi! J'aime aussi la musique.
A: Moi, je n'aime pas la musique rap.
B: Pas moi. C'est super!

Cultural Note: "Moi aussi" and "Moi non plus" are very common and useful phrases in French conversation. They are a concise way to show you agree or disagree with someone's sentiment. This is an easy way to sound more like a native speaker!

Task: You'll participate in a "speed-dating" style preference exchange. With a new partner every few minutes, you'll ask about their likes and dislikes, finding common ground and differences.

Episode 14: Weather & Plans

Objective: You will be able to talk about the weather and make future plans based on the forecast. This is a crucial skill for daily conversation!

  • Weather expressions: Quel temps fait-il ? (What's the weather like?), Il fait beau / mauvais. (The weather is nice / bad.), Il pleut. (It's raining.), Il neige. (It's snowing.), Il y a du soleil / du vent / des nuages. (It's sunny / windy / cloudy.), Il fait chaud / froid. (It's hot / cold.).
  • Making plans based on weather: S'il fait beau, on sort. (If the weather is nice, we'll go out.), S'il fait mauvais, on reste à la maison. (If the weather is bad, we'll stay home.), On peut... (We can...).
  • Seasons: Le printemps (spring), l'été (summer), l'automne (autumn), l'hiver (winter).

Dialogue Example:
A: Quel temps fait-il aujourd'hui?
B: Il fait beau! Il y a du soleil.
A: Super! S'il fait beau, on va à la plage?
B: Oui, bonne idée!

Cultural Note: In France and other French-speaking countries, talking about the weather is a very common topic for small talk, especially when greeting a neighbor or a shopkeeper. It's a great way to start a conversation with a stranger.

Task: You will be given a set of weather cards. You will "report" the weather for the day and then suggest an activity that matches the weather, such as "S'il fait chaud, on va à la plage!"

Episode 15: Recent Past

Objective: You will learn to talk about recent past actions using the passé composé. You'll be able to describe what you did yesterday or last weekend.

  • Time expressions: Hier (yesterday), avant-hier (the day before yesterday), le week-end dernier (last weekend), la semaine dernière (last week), le mois dernier (last month).
  • Past actions (common verbs with 'avoir'): J'ai travaillé, j'ai mangé, j'ai vu, j'ai écouté, j'ai parlé, j'ai visité, j'ai regardé, j'ai fait.
  • Past actions (verbs with 'être'): Je suis allé(e) (I went), je suis resté(e) (I stayed), je suis arrivé(e) (I arrived), je suis parti(e) (I left).
  • Giving opinions: C'était bien / C'était super / C'était fantastique. (It was good/great/fantastic.), C'était nul / C'était terrible. (It was rubbish/terrible.).

Dialogue Example:
A: Qu'est-ce que tu as fait hier?
B: Hier, j'ai travaillé et j'ai regardé un film. C'était super!
A: Et le week-end dernier?
B: Je suis allé(e) au restaurant. C'était très bon.

Cultural Note: In French, the passé composé is the most common way to talk about a specific event in the past. It's used much more frequently in everyday conversation than the simple past tense. Mastering this will allow you to tell stories about your life.

Task: You will sit with a partner and tell them about your weekend. You'll describe what you did, where you went, and how it was, using your new verbs and adjectives.

Episode 16: Near Future

Objective: You will be able to express future plans using the "near future" tense (aller + infinitive). This allows you to talk about what you are going to do.

  • Verbs: Je vais voyager, je vais lire, je vais faire du shopping, je vais me reposer, je vais manger, je vais regarder un film, je vais dormir.
  • Questions: Qu'est-ce que tu vas faire ? (What are you going to do?), Où est-ce que tu vas aller ? (Where are you going to go?), On va se voir demain ? (Are we going to see each other tomorrow?).
  • Time expressions: Après le cours, je vais... (After class, I'm going to...), ce soir (this evening), ce week-end (this weekend), la semaine prochaine (next week).

Dialogue Example:
A: Qu'est-ce que tu vas faire ce week-end?
B: Je vais me reposer et je vais lire un livre.
A: Tu ne vas pas au cinéma?
B: Non, je vais rester à la maison.

Cultural Note: The "near future" tense is very simple to use and is the most common way to talk about upcoming plans. It's a great "LEGO block" to build on to talk about future vacations, plans with friends, and upcoming events.

Task: You and a partner will plan your next week's schedule. You'll use your new knowledge of days of the week and future tense to fill out a shared calendar with different activities.

Episode 17: Describing People

Objective: You will be able to give simple physical and personality descriptions of people, using adjectives to describe friends, family, or even movie characters.

  • Physical Appearance: Il/Elle est grand(e), petit(e), mince, gros(se), fort(e), beau/belle, jeune, vieux/vieille.
  • Hair & Eyes: Il/Elle a les cheveux bruns, blonds, noirs, roux, courts, longs, frisés, raides. Il/Elle a les yeux bleus, verts, marron, noirs.
  • Personality: Il/Elle est gentil(le), drôle, timide, intelligent(e), sympa, sociable, bavard(e), sérieux/sérieuse.
  • Accessories: Il/Elle porte des lunettes. (He/She wears glasses.), un chapeau (a hat), une robe (a dress).

Dialogue Example:
A: C'est qui, cette personne?
B: C'est mon ami, Marc. Il est grand et il a les cheveux bruns. Il est très drôle et gentil.
A: J'aime bien sa veste.

Cultural Note: Just like with nationalities, adjectives in French must agree with the noun they describe. For example, "intelligent" for a male and "intelligente" for a female. This is something that you will practice repeatedly to make it a natural part of your speaking.

Task: You will play a "Who Am I?" game. One person will think of a famous person and describe them to their partner. The partner has to guess who it is based on the description.

Episode 18: Shopping for Clothes

Objective: You will learn key vocabulary for clothing and colors, and be able to ask for specific items and sizes in a store.

  • Clothing items: Un pull (a sweater), un pantalon (pants), une jupe (a skirt), une robe (a dress), une chemise (a shirt), un t-shirt, des chaussures (shoes), des chaussettes (socks), un manteau (a coat).
  • Colors: Bleu(e) (blue), rouge (red), vert(e) (green), jaune (yellow), noir(e) (black), blanc(he) (white), gris(e) (gray), rose (pink), violet(te) (purple), marron (brown).
  • Sizes & Materials: Quelle taille ? (What size?), C'est en coton, en laine, en soie. (It's made of cotton, wool, silk.), C'est de quelle couleur ? (What color is it?).
  • Trying on clothes: J'essaie cette robe. (I'll try on this dress.), La cabine d'essayage (the fitting room), Ça me va bien/mal. (It fits me well/badly.).

Dialogue Example:
A: Bonjour! Je cherche un t-shirt noir, s'il vous plaît.
B: Quelle taille?
A: Je porte du L.
B: Voici. Vous voulez essayer?

Cultural Note: In smaller boutiques and shops, it's very common to be greeted by the shopkeeper when you enter. A simple "Bonjour" is expected. They will often ask if you need help with "Vous cherchez quelque chose?" ("Are you looking for something?").

Task: You will role-play as a customer and a sales assistant in a French boutique. You'll ask for different items of clothing and practice trying them on and giving your opinion.

Episode 19: Hobbies & Free Time

Objective: You will be able to talk about your hobbies and activities, allowing you to connect with people by discussing your shared interests.

  • Hobbies: Qu'est-ce que tu fais pendant ton temps libre ? (What do you do in your free time?), J'aime faire du sport, du vélo, de la natation, du yoga, de la danse.
  • Playing games/instruments: Je joue au football, au tennis, au basket. Je joue de la guitare, du piano.
  • Media consumption: J'écoute de la musique, je lis des livres, je regarde des films, des séries.
  • Travel & Outdoors: Je voyage, je vais à la plage, je fais de la randonnée.

Dialogue Example:
A: Qu'est-ce que tu fais pendant ton temps libre?
B: J'aime faire du sport. Je joue au football et je fais du vélo.
A: Moi, j'écoute de la musique et je lis des livres.
B: C'est bien!

Cultural Note: In France, there is often a strong distinction between work and personal life. Conversations with colleagues and friends often shift to what you do for fun once work is done. Be prepared to talk about your free time and hobbies!

Task: You will discuss your favorite free time activities with a partner. You'll find out what you have in common and what you do differently, using the phrases you've learned.

Episode 20: Final Review & Recap

Objective: You will consolidate all the A1 topics you've learned to demonstrate a basic conversational ability in French.

  • Expressions: All previous lessons' key expressions and vocabulary, including: Greetings & Farewells, Nationalities & Places, Numbers & Time, Ordering at a Café, Classroom Language, Family & Descriptions, Jobs, Daily Routine, Making Plans, Directions, Home & Housing, Shopping, Hobbies, and Talking about the past and future.

Dialogue Example:
A: Salut! Ça va?
B: Ça va bien. Et toi? Je suis allé au cinéma hier.
A: Ah, super! Moi, je vais aller au café ce week-end. Je voudrais un croissant et un thé.
B: C'est une bonne idée!

Cultural Note: The goal of the A1 level is to have very basic, functional conversations. You'll be able to handle simple interactions with locals, ask basic questions, and understand common replies. This is a big step on your journey to fluency!

Task: You will have a final "Can Do" challenge. You and a partner will have a 5-minute conversation using only the French you've learned over the 20 lessons. You will introduce yourselves, talk about your family, hobbies, jobs, and what you did last weekend.
